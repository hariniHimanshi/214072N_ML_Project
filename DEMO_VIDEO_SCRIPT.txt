SAY:

   Heavy Rain Prediction project.

   The goal of this project is to predict whether the NEXT week in

   15 millimetres of total precipitation — using 18 years of historical
   weather data from NOAA.

   I built an XGBoost Classifier and wrapped it in this interactive

================================================================================
[00:25 - 01:00]  SIDEBAR — Explaining the Inputs
================================================================================

ACTION: Point to / hover over the sidebar inputs one by one.

SAY:
  "On the left side, we have the input panel. The app only asks for
   RAW weather observations — it does all the feature engineering
   automatically behind the scenes.

   At the top, there's a DATE PICKER. When I pick a date, the app
   automatically derives the Week of Year, Month, and seasonal
   sin/cos features — you can see them appear as small blue pills
   right below.

   Next, under 'This Week's Observations', I enter the current week's
   total rainfall in millimetres, the average, highest, and lowest
   temperatures, and the number of rainy days. Notice the Temp Range
   pill that appears automatically — it's computed from max minus min.

   Below that, under 'Last Week's Observations', I enter last week's
   rainfall, average temperature, and rainy days.

   And finally, under 'Two Weeks Ago', just the rainfall and
   average temperature.

   That's only 10 raw inputs total — the app computes all 14 model
   features automatically. And predictions update LIVE as I change
   any value — there's no submit button needed.

   In a real-world deployment, these inputs wouldn't need to be
   entered manually — they could be fetched automatically from a
   weather API like OpenWeatherMap or the Sri Lanka Meteorological
   Department's API. But for this demo, we enter them manually to
   show how the model responds to different scenarios."

================================================================================
[01:45 - 02:30]  SHAP WATERFALL — Explaining the Prediction
================================================================================

ACTION: Point to the SHAP waterfall chart on the right side of the screen.

SAY:
  "The bar chart here is called a SHAP Waterfall — it shows exactly
   WHY the model made this prediction.

   The BLUE bars are features pushing the prediction TOWARD a dry week.
   The RED bars push it TOWARD a rainy week.

   In this case, you can see that features like low Last Week Rainfall
   — meaning little rain last week — and the seasonal timing
   in February — which is the dry season — are the dominant factors
   pushing this firmly into the 'Dry' category.

   This is what makes SHAP so powerful for explainability. We are not
   just getting a black-box answer — we can see exactly which inputs
   drove the model's decision, and in which direction."
